# Layout Recipes

Use these recipes to turn the aesthetic into reliable compositions.

## Website and landing-page recipes

### Recipe A: Type wall hero
Use when the brand or message can carry a big typographic statement.

Composition:
- Set a 12-column grid.
- Let the main word or phrase span 7 to 11 columns.
- Break the line intentionally to create a strong typographic silhouette.
- Place one small metadata cluster near the top edge or side edge.
- Place one concise supporting paragraph in a lower corner.
- Reserve one or two columns as negative space.

Works well for:
- studios
- products with a bold point of view
- decks that open with a thesis

Common failure:
- adding too much body copy below the hero and weakening the impact

### Recipe B: Giant numeral plus metadata
Use when the content can be divided into issues, versions, sections, or a sequence.

Composition:
- Use an oversized `01`, `02`, `03`, or year marker as the dominant move.
- Let the numeral overlap multiple sections or occupy a large fixed zone.
- Pair it with a heading block and a short descriptor.
- Add a precise metadata stack with dates, credits, or categories.
- Use a single accent field or rule to support the number.

Works well for:
- portfolios
- annual or edition-based content
- section dividers in decks

Common failure:
- treating the numeral like decoration instead of a compositional anchor

### Recipe C: Modular geometry hero
Use when the brand can be expressed through simple abstract forms.

Composition:
- Build a grid of circles, semicircles, capsules, or blocks.
- Let one accent-colored shape break the neutral field.
- Keep the headline smaller or secondary if the geometry is dominant.
- Align the geometry precisely to the columns and rows.
- Use captions or labels to ground the abstraction.

Works well for:
- design studios
- architecture, culture, or system-oriented products
- posters translated into digital landing pages

Common failure:
- using shapes that look decorative rather than structural

### Recipe D: Image crop plus type rail
Use when photography or a single visual asset matters.

Composition:
- Crop the image hard to the grid.
- Place a vertical or horizontal typographic rail along one side.
- Overlay only a minimal amount of text on the image itself.
- Use captions, credits, and descriptors in empty grid cells.
- Maintain strong black, white, and accent contrast.

Works well for:
- fashion, culture, music, editorial brands
- decks with case studies or campaigns

Common failure:
- using full-bleed image wallpaper with floating text not anchored to the grid

### Recipe E: Visible grid field
Use when the system itself should be part of the visual message.

Composition:
- Draw or imply a visible poster grid.
- Place text blocks and shapes as if they occupy specific grid cells.
- Use thin rules or lines to reveal the structure.
- Keep the content sparse so the grid remains readable.

Works well for:
- design systems
- process-oriented brands
- educational or manifesto pages

Common failure:
- showing the grid but not actually aligning content to it

## Supporting section recipes

### Manifesto section
- Use one strong statement across 5 to 8 columns.
- Place a smaller paragraph in a non-adjacent block.
- Add a section number.
- Leave at least one major empty area.

### Feature matrix
- Use rules, labels, and typographic columns instead of colorful cards.
- Group features by column or band.
- Keep icon use minimal or omit entirely.
- Let one row or column receive the accent color.

### Quote section
- Set one short quote very large.
- Place attribution in small metadata.
- Keep the rest of the section almost empty.

### Case-study section
- Lead with an index number and project title.
- Use one hard image crop.
- Place technical notes, outcomes, or descriptors in a compact stack.
- Avoid many boxed callouts.

### CTA section
- Keep the CTA severe and typographic.
- Use one command or invitation.
- Support it with a link, arrow, rule, or simple rectangular button.
- End decisively rather than with marketing fluff.

## Slide recipes

### Title slide
- Make one headline or key word very large.
- Add a small metadata line with presenter, date, or context.
- Keep everything else empty.

### Section divider
- Use a large numeral or word.
- Anchor it to the grid edge.
- Add a small subtitle or descriptor.

### Statement slide
- Use one sentence only.
- Set it at a size that feels almost too large.
- Allow line breaks that create shape.

### Framework slide
- Use rules and typographic compartments.
- Avoid colorful infographic styling.
- Keep labels crisp and aligned.

### Comparison slide
- Split the page into grid bands.
- Use one clear axis and one accent highlight.
- Keep the visual language flat and typographic.

### Closing slide
- Return to a strong typographic or numeric move.
- Use a concise call to action or final thesis.
- Include contact or credit information as a small aligned block.

## Section sequencing guidance
For a landing page, this order usually works well:
1. hero
2. thesis or manifesto
3. feature or capability structure
4. proof or case studies
5. detail or process section
6. closing CTA or footer credits

For a slide deck, this order usually works well:
1. title
2. thesis
3. context
4. framework
5. evidence or examples
6. conclusion
7. closing slide
